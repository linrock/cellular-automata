Cellular automata

A cellular automaton is a grid of cells where each cell changes over time based on a set of rules and the state of its neighbors. The state of a grid in the next point in time is called a generation. Simple rules can lead to complex patterns.

One-dimensional cellular automata

The world is a 1D row of cells. In each new generation of the world, the state of a cell changes based on its current state and the states of its neighbors.

We can visualize the evolution of 1D cellular automata over time by adding new generations to the top or bottom of a grid. If we do this continuously over time, we'll see a pattern scrolling vertically.

Elementary cellular automata

The simplest automata are known as elementary cellular automata (ECA). Each cell has 2 possible states: 0 or 1. The state of each cell in the next generation depends only on 3 values: its current state and the state of its two immediate neighbors.

Since there are 23 = 8 orientations of the 3 values, and there are 2 states for a cell, there are 28 = 256 possible rules for determining the new state of a cell in the next generation of the world.

A common way to view the behavior of a rule is to initialize the first generation with a single 1 in the middle. Some rules will lead to simple or symmetric patterns while some will lead to chaotic patterns. Rule 30 leads to aperiodic and chaotic behavior.

ECAs behave differently when cells are initialized with random cells. For example, with rule 106, starting with a single 1 in the middle leads to only a diagonal line. However, when the cells start with random states, chaotic behavior arises.

Two-dimensional cellular automata

The world is a 2D grid of cells. In each new generation, the state of a cell may change based on its current state and the states of its surrounding neighbors.

We can visualize the evoluation of 2D cellular automata over time by updating the states of the cells on the 2D grid with the new cell states from each new generation.

Conway's Game of Life

Created by mathematician John Conway in 1970, this is perhaps the most well-known cellular automata. Cells have two possible states: live or dead. New generations of the world consider only the 8 immediate neighbors of each cell.

In each generation, the state of the world is generated based on these rules:

  • A dead cell with 3 neighbors becomes live
  • A live cell with 2 or 3 neighbors stays live
  • All other live cells become dead

Life-like cellular automata

Cellular automata are considered Life-like if they're 2D, cells have 2 possible states, and change based on their 8 immediate neighbors. The rules by which cell states change may differ. A common notation for rules is written as Bx/Sy, where:

  • B = Birth - dead cells with these numbers of neighbors become live
  • S = Survival - live cells with these numbers of neighbors stay live
  • x and y are strings of digits from 1 to 8

For example, the rules of the Game of Life can be written as: B3/S23

Day and Night

In Day and Night, each cell has two possible states: on or off. The on and off states in the world are symmetric. If the state of all cells in the world are inverted, the future world is the inverted version of the original.

Since this follows similar rules as the Game of Life, it's considered a Life-like cellular automata.

New generations of Day and Night worlds follow these rules:

  • An off cell with 3, 6, 7, or 8 neighbors becomes on
  • An on cell with 3, 4, 6, 7, or 8 neighbors becomes off
  • All other on cells become off

The rules can be written as: B3678/S34678

Mazectric

Also a Life-like cellular automata, this is an example of a maze generation algorithm. When initialized with random values in the center, cells will expand outwards towards the edges until a steady state is reached.

In some cases, oscillator patterns will appear, where 2x2 cells will continuously switch between on and off states.

Cyclic cellular automata

This is a multi-state system where the state of a cell changes if enough neighboring cells are in a state that win over it. The tri-state version is also known as "Rock, paper, scissors". For example, if a scissors cell is surrounded by enough rock cells, then the scissors cell becomes a rock in the next generation.

When initialized with random values, this system can reach a steady state of spiral waves.

Trails in 2D

In the Game of Life, if we instead fade a cell from live to dead over a few generations, moving patterns in the world will appear to leave trails behind them.

Elementary automaton + Game of Life

The bottom half of the world is an elementary cellular automaton. As each generation reaches the middle, it influences the edge of the top half of the world, which follows the Game of Life.

2x elementary automata + Game of Life

The top and bottom fourths of the world are two different elementary cellular automata. The middle half of the world follows the rules of the Game of Life.
