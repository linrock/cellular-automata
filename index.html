Cellular automata

A cellular automaton is a grid of cells where each cell changes over time based on a set of rules and the state of its neighbors. The state of a grid in the next point in time is called a generation. Simple rules can lead to complex patterns.

One-dimensional cellular automata

The world is a 1D row of cells. Each new generation considers the state of a cell along with the states of its neighbors to its left and right.

We can visualize the evolution of 1D cellular automata over time by adding new generations to the top or bottom of a grid. If we do this continuously over time, we'll see a pattern scrolling vertically.

Elementary cellular automata

The simplest automata are known as elementary cellular automata (ECA). Each cell has two possible states. The state of a cell in the next generation depends on its current state and the state of its two immediate neighbors on its left and right.

Two-dimensional cellular automata

The world is a 2D grid of cells. In each new generation, the state of a cell may change based on its current state and the states of its surrounding neighbors on the 2D grid.

We can visualize the evoluation of 2D cellular automata over time by updating the states of the cells on the 2D grid with the new cell states from each new generation.

Conway's Game of Life

Cells have two possible states: live or dead. New generations of the world consider only the 8 immediate neighbors of each cell. In each generation, the state of the world is generated based on these rules:

  • A dead cell with 3 neighbors becomes a live cell
  • A live cell with 2 or 3 neighbors stays the same
  • All other live cells become dead cells

Day and Night

This automata is similar to the Game of Life. Each cell also has two possible states: on or off. The state of the world is generated based on these rules:

  • An off cell with 3, 6, 7, or 8 neighbors becomes on
  • An on cell with 3, 4, 6, 7, or 8 neighbors becomes off
  • All other on cells become off

An interesting property of Day and Night is the symmetry of the on and off states. If the state of all cells in the world are inverted, the future world is the inverted version of the original.

Visual experiments

Trails in 2D

In the Game of Life, if we instead fade a cell from live to dead over a few generations, moving patterns in the world will appear to leave trails behind them.

Elementary automaton + Game of Life

The bottom half of the world is an elementary cellular automaton. As each generation reaches the middle, it influences the top half of the world, which is governed by the Game of Life.

2x elementary automata + Game of Life

The top and bottom thirds of the world are two different elementary cellular automata. The middle third of the world follows the Game of Life.
