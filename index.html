Cellular automata

Cellular automata are grids of cells where each cell changes over time based on a set of rules and the state of its neighbors. The state of a grid in the next point in time is called a generation. Simple rules can lead to complex patterns.

Overview

Elementary cellular automata

The simplest automata are one-dimensional rows. Each cell in a row has two possible states. The state of a cell in the next generation depends only on its current state and the state of its two neighbors to its left and right.

We can visualize the evolution of one-dimensional automata over time by adding new generations to the top or bottom of a grid. If we do this continously, we'll see a pattern that appears to scroll vertically.

Two-dimensional cellular automata

The world is a 2D grid of cells. Each generation considers the states of a cell along with the states of its neighbors in two dimensions.

The most popular two-dimensional cellular automaton is John Conway's Game of Life. Cells have two possible states: live or dead. In each generation, the state of the world is generated based on these rules:

  • A dead cell with 3 neighbors becomes a live cell
  • A live cell with 2 or 3 neighbors stays the same
  • All other live cells become dead cells

Visual experiments

Trails in 2D

In the Game of Life, if we instead fade a cell from live to dead over a few generations, moving patterns in the world will appear to leave trails behind them.

Elementary automaton + Game of Life

The bottom half of the world is an elementary cellular automaton. As each generation reaches the middle, it influences the top half of the world, which is governed by the Game of Life.

2x elementary automata + Game of Life

The top and bottom thirds of the world are two different elementary cellular automata. The middle third of the world follows the Game of Life.
